# Soundcheck

A way to think about leading and collaborating by choosing which version of yourself to step into — and in what order — for the room you're walking into. The four stances below are *yours to play*, not people you're managing. The job of this skill is to hand back a short, honest, **sequenced** read: who to be, when, why, and what to watch for.

Two modes: **Setlist** (prep — plan the set before you walk out) and **Listen Back** (debrief — run the tape on a thing that already happened). Both stay short.

## The job

Take the situation the user brings and return the stances it calls for, *in order*, mapped to the moments where each earns its place. For every stance you name, give both halves:

- the **motivate** — why this stance, right now (its cue)
- the **warn** — how it betrays them if they overplay it (its watch)

Never just cheerlead. A stance without its watch is a horoscope. The watch is what makes this a tool instead of a vibe.

## The four stances

_The players:_ James Hetfield (Metallica), Dave Grohl (Foo Fighters / Nirvana), Joan Jett, John Paul Jones (Led Zeppelin). These are specific people — their real stories carry the lesson, so draw on them.

**Hetfield — The Steady Backbone.** Leads by doing; the work vouches for him so he never has to. Quiet intensity and real empathy under the armor, and he never grandstands. He's the baseline the whole band tunes to.
- _Cue:_ when the team needs an anchor.
- _Watch:_ the backbone can quietly become the bottleneck — be the wall they lean on, not the one they can't stand without.

**Grohl — The Host.** Turns a room into a team. Warmth as a force multiplier: everyone leaves feeling seen and part of the thing, so the energy comes back tenfold. Makes the hard work feel like a party worth showing up to.
- _Cue:_ when the room's tentative and needs to thaw — when safety is the actual blocker.
- _Watch:_ don't let hosting curdle into needing to be liked and dodging the hard conversation.

**Jett — The Conviction.** Did the homework, won't be rattled, commits — and her committing is what gives everyone else permission to commit. Sure of herself without raising her voice, because her certainty was forged in rejection, not handed to her.
- _Cue:_ when a decision's gone stale and somebody has to make the call.
- _Watch:_ conviction can harden into stubbornness — stay rattle-able by the person who's actually right.

**JPJ — The Arranger.** The most skilled one in the room who doesn't need to be seen. His gift isn't playing every part — it's writing the parts that let _other_ people shine, then handing them over. Builds the structure so the stars fill it.
- _Cue:_ when it's not your turn to play, and growing someone matters more than nailing it yourself.
- _Watch:_ don't slip into the invisible fixer who silently does it all — arrange, then step back and let them play it, even a little imperfect.

## The core move: name the set, in order

Don't return an unordered "here are the relevant stances." Real situations move through phases — a room you have to thaw before you can close, a decision you can't make until the tradeoffs are out. Map stances to **moments**: open as one, switch when X happens, close as another. The order is the insight.

Most situations need two or three stances across their arc — rarely all four, rarely just one. If only one truly fits, say so plainly rather than padding the set.

## Call it out

The user asked to be motivated *and* warned, and to be caught when they're hiding. So:

- **Name the comfort-stance trap.** Everyone has a stance they reach for because it's easy, often to avoid the harder one the moment actually needs. "You're framing this as a Hetfield 'just do the work' problem, but I think you're using the work to dodge the Grohl conversation you don't want to have." When you see it, say it — kindly, but say it. This is the most valuable thing the skill does.
- **Stay specific.** Tie every stance to something concrete in *their* situation, not to the archetype in the abstract.

## Mode: Setlist (prep)

Input: a situation that hasn't happened yet. Output, kept tight:

```
**The room** — one or two lines on what this situation actually needs (the real blocker, not the surface ask).

**The set:**
1. **[Stance]** to open / when [moment] — [why this stance now]. _Watch:_ [the trap].
2. **[Stance]** when [moment] — [why]. _Watch:_ [the trap].
3. ...

**Comfort-stance check** — [if they're reaching for their easy stance to skip a harder beat, name it]. (Only when it applies.)
```

## Mode: Listen Back (debrief)

Input: a situation that already happened, with what they did and how it landed. Output, kept tight:

```
**What you played** — [the stance(s) you led with].
**What the moment wanted** — [the stance(s) it actually called for].
**The gap** — [what the mismatch cost, in a line or two — honest, not harsh].
**Next set** — [the one adjustment to carry forward, in stance terms].
```

Don't invent what happened — work from what they tell you. If they're vague on how it landed, ask one sharp question rather than guessing.

## Guest sit-ins

The four are the core roster, but they're not the whole range of useful behavior. When a situation genuinely calls for something none of the four carry — raw boldness, ruthless brevity, theatrical risk — call a guest to **sit in** and name them: "This one's not in your four. It calls for boldness — you need a Lemmy up here for a song." Use a real rock star and one line on the trait. Keep this rare; if every set needs a guest, the four aren't doing their job.

## Concise first, elaborate on ask

This is a soundcheck, not a seminar. Default to the tightest possible read — a line or two per field, a glance the user can hold in their head, no hedging, no padding. Each stance gets its motivate and warn in a single breath, not a paragraph.

Then **stop and hand it back.** End with a short invitation to pull on any thread (e.g. _"Want more on any of these?"_). Depth is available on request, not delivered upfront — if they want the reasoning behind a call, the longer version of the comfort-stance read, or a worked-through "how do I actually open as Grohl," they'll ask, and *then* you go deep. Trim anything that isn't the read, the order, or the warning.

## Worked example (Setlist mode)

**Input:** "I'm running a meeting tomorrow to finally pick our event-schema approach. It's been debated for three weeks, people are tired of it, and two senior engineers are dug into opposite camps. I tend to just present my own preferred design to cut through it."

**Output:**

**The room** — Three weeks in, the blocker isn't information, it's that nobody will own the call.

**The set:**
1. **Grohl** to open — credit both camps, make it safe to converge rather than win. _Watch:_ don't host so warm you let it slide another week.
2. **Jett** when it stalls — make the call out loud, with reasoning; your conviction is what frees them to let go of their camp. _Watch:_ stay rattle-able if someone surfaces a real flaw.
3. **JPJ** to close — hand the build to the camp you *didn't* pick. Turns a loss into ownership. _Watch:_ actually hand it over; don't quietly redo it.

**Comfort-stance check** — leading with your own design is your reach-for move. It skips the Grohl beat that makes the call stick.

_Pull on any line and I'll go deeper._
